The Oilrig was built to feel labyrinthian, unfamiliar and uninviting. We wanted to capture the precarity and challenge in navigating such an environment, as well as embrace the chaos this would lend to combat, with many different ranges of overlapping sightlines to consider. I was responsible for each of the blockouts of this map, particularly its earlier developments, and helped oversee its interations into the final version that ships with the game today.

The map is divided into 3 major sections: the superstructure (underside), interior and exterior. We intended each of these sections to be self-contained so they could function as their own Level Layout, and we built the higher difficulty layouts around player traversal from one of these sections to another. Each of these sections has a unique gameplay feel. The superstructure is precarious and features overlapping long sightlines with a high degree of verticality. Players need to cover each other methodically or they will be attacked from numerous unpredictable angles. The interior is built around claustrophobic and simple hallways, injecting that adrenaline shot of CQC into the mix. The exterior is much more straightforward, with simpler long sightlines that enable a more controlled and simple combat feel.

One design challenge with the map is that moving between the 3 major sections can be quite confusing. Though these sections are fairly intuitive internally, we noticed that players tend to get lost when trying to move between them. Due to the layered and twisting nature of the level, it can become hard to identify the major routes in and out of a section. If I could take another stab at the layout, I would reconstruct the "transitional areas" to be even larger focal points of each section. Additionally, we really struggled with cluttered and overwhelming sightlines, particularly in the superstructure. It was a common occurrence for players to not understand where they were getting shot from, or to get hit from an unanticipated angle. This was a positive point in keeping the level unpredictable and dangerous, but went too far to the point where it probably became slightly irksome for players.

I do think the Oilrig shines on a macro level. It feels really satisfying to clear out the Moonpool, and then tackle the Container Maze and push towards extraction by the Bridge / Helipad. It gives you a rewarding sense of progression through a series of distinct encounters. I also think the map makes clever use of verticality, moreso than any other Rogue Point level. Despite the level's total high degree of vertical sightlines, we stuck to dividing each major area into only two levels: "upper" and “lower”. Thus, at any given position, a player will usually only be shot from above or below; never both. This is one of the Oilrig's little design secrets that keeps its gameplay tight despite the layout being extremely dense and complex.
